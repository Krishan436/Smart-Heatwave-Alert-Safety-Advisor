Science Behind Heat Index • Emergency Warnings

The Science of Apparent Temperature

When the human body overheats, it relies on the evaporation of sweat to cool down. However, when humidity is high, the moisture level in the surrounding air is already elevated, which slows down the rate of evaporation.

Because sweat cannot evaporate efficiently, the body is unable to shed excess heat as effectively. Consequently, you feel much hotter than the actual thermometer reading. This combined effect is defined as the Heat Index or Apparent Temperature.

Safety Threshold Categories (NOAA Standards)

Heat Index Range Severity Category Associated Danger & Health Hazards
Below 27°C (80°F) Safe / Normal Low risk of heat stress. Ideal weather conditions.
27°C - 32°C (80°F - 90°F) Caution Fatigue is possible with prolonged exposure and activity.
32°C - 41°C (90°F - 105°F) Extreme Caution Heat cramps, exhaustion, and sunstroke are possible.
41°C - 54°C (105°F - 130°F) Danger Heat exhaustion, cramps, and sunstroke likely with activity.
Over 54°C (130°F) Extreme Danger Heatstroke / sunstroke is imminent. Immediate intervention required.

Heat Stroke vs. Heat Exhaustion Symptoms

Heat Exhaustion (Act Immediately)

Your body is struggling to stay cool.

  • Faintness or dizziness
  • Excessive sweating
  • Cool, pale, clammy skin
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Rapid, weak pulse
  • Muscle cramps
Action: Move to an air-conditioned room, drink water, take a cool shower, or use cool compresses.

Heat Stroke (Medical Emergency)

Call Emergency Services (911 / 112) Immediately!

  • Throbbing headache
  • No sweating (dry skin) or excessive sweating
  • Body temperature above 39.4°C (103°F)
  • Red, hot, dry skin
  • Rapid, strong pulse
  • May lose consciousness
Action: Call emergency medical services. Move the victim to cool shades, and cool them with cold water/ice packs until help arrives. Do not give fluids to drink if unconscious.
